**Sweden's parliamentary motion for a national Bitcoin reserve names two countries by name as the comparators Sweden fears falling behind.**

**Finland and Norway.**

**Finland has no public position on a question Sweden has named Finland into.**

Today I'm publishing a policy whitepaper that proposes one.

—

In October 2024, two members of the Swedish Riksdag filed Motion 2024/25:1232 — a formal parliamentary proposal for a Swedish sovereign Bitcoin reserve. The reasoning section of that motion explicitly says Sweden "risks falling behind Finland and Norway if it stays too cautious."

I read that motion twice when I first encountered it. The second time, I realized something uncomfortable: Sweden was citing Finland as an example of a country moving — but Finland has no public framework, no parliamentary motion, no Suomen Pankki working paper, and no operator-authored proposal on this question.

Finland is being cited as a leader on a topic on which Finland has not actually led.

That gap is what this whitepaper closes.

—

**What's in the document**

Title: *Finland's Path to a Sovereign Bitcoin Reserve: Operating Model, Custody, Political Sequencing*

~12 pages. English, with a Finnish translation forthcoming. Open-source on GitHub. Licensed CC-BY 4.0 so anyone can quote, adapt, or fork.

The proposal is three sequential tracks:

**Track A — Establish a published governance framework for state-held digital assets.** Finland already custodies digital assets that arrive through law-enforcement seizure. The operational reality is fragmented across at least three agencies (Tulli, Verohallinto, the relevant prosecutorial offices); liquidation decisions are made on a recurring basis without published policy; no defined cadence reports flows to Eduskunta. Track A does not propose growing this footprint — it proposes governing it. A single named authority, a published framework, a quarterly transparency report, a deferral of further liquidation pending parliamentary review. No new monetary policy. No purchases. No parliamentary authorization beyond an interagency MoU. Worth doing on its own merits, even if Tracks B and C are never adopted.

**Track B — Establish the legal mechanism for permanent sovereign accumulation.** A one-time amendment to the Confiscation Act (Pakkokeinolaki) allows Bitcoin obtained through law-enforcement seizure, after due process, to enter a permanent sovereign reserve rather than being liquidated to euros. Budget-neutral. Mirrors the U.S. Strategic Bitcoin Reserve framework (March 2025) and parallels the Swedish opposition's October 2024 proposal. The point is the *mechanism* and the legal precedent it establishes — not the immediate flow rate.

**Track C — Strategic allocation.** A 0.5% allocation of FX reserves to Bitcoin, phased over five years. Sized so that even a 90% drawdown represents less than 0.05% of total reserves. Conditional on Tracks A and B succeeding. Debated openly in Eduskunta.

The whitepaper also covers:

→ A three-tier custody architecture (Suomen Pankki cold storage + institutional partners + independent proof-of-reserves audit)
→ Political sequencing framed for every major Finnish party — Kokoomus, Perussuomalaiset, Keskusta, SDP, Vihreät, Vasemmistoliitto, RKP
→ The five structural reasons Finland is well-positioned: cheap nuclear electricity, cold climate, engineering pedigree (Linux, Nokia, Martti Malmi), high-trust governance (#1 Transparency International), mature MiCA implementation
→ Replies to the five most common counter-arguments (volatility, ECB, illicit use, legitimacy signaling, the Dalio objection)
→ Three concrete moves Finland can take in 2026 under €500,000 combined cost and with no new legislation

**Why now**

The international policy environment changed between October 2024 and May 2026 in ways that are easy to miss if you don't follow the beat:

- The U.S. has signalled the Treasury purchase mechanism is imminent (May 2026).
- Sweden's motion is under Finance Committee review.
- The Czech National Bank Governor publicly discussed Bitcoin allocation in January 2025.
- Bhutan's sovereign reserve is actively operational at ~$252M.
- Switzerland's citizen referendum failed in May 2026 — confirming that this is a top-down policy question, not a bottom-up initiative.
- The ECB is silent because the composition of member-state reserves is a national competence, not a monetary policy question.

The 18-month window opens with Sweden's filing and closes with the autumn 2026 Eduskunta budget cycle. Within that window, Finland can take a deliberate position. After that window, Finland's position is defined by other actors.

—

**Why I wrote this in personal capacity, and why I'm publishing it open-source**

Three reasons.

First, the credibility check on a document like this happens on GitHub, not on a corporate website. Bitcoiners and engineers read source code, citation files, commit history, and license terms. Publishing on GitHub passes that check in a way a PDF on a marketing page does not.

Second, CC-BY 4.0 is the right license for a policy document. It lets MPs, journalists, central-bank staff, and other governments quote freely with attribution. It removes legal friction from press coverage. It enables forks for Sweden, Norway, Estonia, or any country that wants to adapt the political-sequencing chapter for their own party landscape.

Third, the source-of-truth being a public Git repo gives independent verification of the "first published on" timestamp. That matters for a document that proposes a policy position. Commit history is harder to retro-fit than a PDF metadata field.

**What I'm hoping for**

This document is a starting point, not a conclusion. The three best outcomes I can imagine, in increasing order of impact:

1. **One Finnish MP or Suomen Pankki staffer replies to my email,** opens a 30-minute conversation, and the conversation goes somewhere useful. (Probability: realistic.)
2. **A working group is convened in the Eduskunta** to produce a non-binding Finnish-authored framework. (Probability: depends on the next 6 months of advocacy.)
3. **The document is forked in Stockholm, Oslo, or Tallinn** and adapted as a starting point for those jurisdictions. (Probability: medium — it's already a public file under CC-BY 4.0.)
