**Operating Model, Custody, Political Sequencing**

## Executive summary

The case rests on four converging facts:

1. **The international policy direction has settled.** The United States established a Strategic Bitcoin Reserve in March 2025. Sweden's opposition filed a parliamentary motion in October 2024. The Czech central-bank governor publicly considered an allocation in January 2025. Switzerland's grassroots referendum failed in May 2026 — top-down paths succeed, bottom-up do not.
2. **Sweden's filing names Finland and Norway by name** as the comparator states it fears falling behind. The position of Nordic leadership on this question is, at this moment, on offer rather than contested.
3. **Finland's structural advantages are rare in combination**: cheap nuclear electricity, cold climate, engineering pedigree (Linux, Nokia, Martti Malmi), high-trust governance (#1 Transparency International), mature MiCA implementation.
4. **The European Central Bank is silent because the question is structurally outside its mandate** — member-state reserve composition is a national competence.

Within this frame the whitepaper proposes three sequential tracks:

1. **Track A — Establish a published governance framework for state-held digital assets.** Finland already custodies digital assets through law-enforcement seizure across at least three agencies, with no published framework, no defined reporting cadence, and no coherent strategic logic. Track A does not propose growing this footprint — it proposes governing it. Zero new monetary policy required.
2. **Track B — Establish the legal mechanism for permanent sovereign accumulation.** A one-time amendment to the Confiscation Act (Pakkokeinolaki) allows seized Bitcoin, after due process, to enter a permanent sovereign reserve rather than being liquidated. Budget-neutral. Mirrors the U.S. Strategic Bitcoin Reserve framework (2025) and parallels the Swedish opposition proposal (2024).
3. **Track C — Strategic allocation.** A 0.5% allocation of FX reserves to Bitcoin, phased over five years. Conditional on Tracks A and B succeeding. Debated openly in Eduskunta.

The full document covers each track in detail, a three-tier custody architecture, political sequencing across every major Finnish party, the structural Finnish edge, replies to the five most common counter-arguments, and three concrete first moves Finland can take in 2026 with no new legislation under €500,000 combined cost.

## Why this document exists

The international policy environment has converged. Sweden has filed. The United States is days away. Switzerland's bottom-up path failed. The ECB is silent because the question is outside its mandate. Finland has no public position on a question Sweden has named Finland into, and the leadership window is open for approximately eighteen months.
