Finland's Path to a Sovereign Bitcoin Reserve

Operating model, custody, political sequencing.

By Jukka Blomberg · Founder, NorthPoint · Helsinki
Published 13 May 2026 · CC-BY 4.0 · Open source on GitHub

Sweden's parliamentary motion for a national Bitcoin reserve names Finland and Norway by name as the comparator states it fears falling behind. The United States has established a Strategic Bitcoin Reserve. The Czech central-bank governor has publicly considered an allocation. Switzerland's grassroots path failed. The European Central Bank is silent — the composition of member-state reserves is a national competence. Finland has no public position on a question Sweden has named Finland into. This whitepaper proposes one.

Three recommendations

  1. Track A — Establish a published governance framework for state-held digital assets. Finland already custodies digital assets via law-enforcement seizure across at least three agencies, with no published framework and no reporting cadence. Track A formalizes the framework the operational reality already implies. Zero new monetary policy required.
  2. Track B — Establish the legal mechanism for permanent sovereign accumulation. A one-time amendment to the Confiscation Act allows seized Bitcoin, after due process, to enter a permanent reserve rather than being liquidated. Budget-neutral. Mirrors the U.S. Strategic Bitcoin Reserve (2025) and the Swedish opposition proposal (2024).
  3. Track C — Strategic allocation. A 0.5% allocation of FX reserves to Bitcoin, phased over five years. Conditional, debated openly in Eduskunta, sized so that even a 90% drawdown is < 0.05% of total reserves.

The case is not ideological — it is structural. The riskier path is doing nothing.

What's inside the whitepaper

  • The complete three-track operating model with what each requires and how each is sequenced.
  • A three-tier custody architecture: Suomen Pankki cold storage, institutional partner custody, and an independent proof-of-reserves audit layer.
  • Political sequencing tailored to each major Finnish party (Kokoomus, Perussuomalaiset, Keskusta, SDP, Vihreät, Vasemmistoliitto, RKP).
  • The Finnish structural edge — cheap nuclear electricity, cold climate, engineering credibility, high-trust governance, mature regulatory framework.
  • Replies to the five most common counter-arguments (volatility, ECB, illicit-use, legitimacy, the Dalio objection).
  • Three concrete moves Finland can take in 2026 with no new legislation, under €500,000 combined cost.

Why this is open source

A PDF on a website is a static artifact. A GitHub repository is a living, version-controlled, citable, forkable record — and it carries credibility in the engineering and Bitcoin communities that read this kind of work most carefully.

The whitepaper is licensed under Creative Commons Attribution 4.0, which means policymakers in Sweden, Norway, Estonia, Denmark, or anywhere else can fork it, adapt the political-sequencing chapter for their own party landscape, and republish. The custody and operating-model chapters are jurisdiction-agnostic.

The repository contains the source markdown, the data behind the figures cited, the build pipeline that produces the PDF, and an issue tracker for corrections, counter-arguments, and translation contributions.
